White Has 20 Possible First Moves in Chess

In the starting position of a chess game, White has exactly 20 legal first moves. This includes:

  • Each of the 8 pawns can advance either 1 square (to the third rank) or 2 squares (to the fourth rank), totaling 16 pawn moves.
  • Each of the 2 knights can move to 2 possible squares each (unblocked by pawns), totaling 4 knight moves.

No other pieces can move on the first turn, as they are blocked by pawns. Below is a complete list of all 20 moves in standard algebraic notation, sorted alphabetically for clarity. For each, I've provided Black's recommended first move at the highest level of play (based on opening theory, grandmaster practice, and engine evaluations like Stockfish). The recommendation focuses on "correct play"—solid, principled responses that aim to equalize or counterattack while following key opening principles: controlling the center, developing pieces, and ensuring king safety. Explanations include why the move is strong, drawing from chess theory as of 2025.

List of White's First Moves and Black's Optimal Responses

  • 1. a3 (Anderssen's Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: This passive move ignores the center, so Black immediately occupies it with a pawn, gaining space and development tempo. At high levels, this leads to easy equality or advantage, as White's a3 is irrelevant early on.
  • 1. a4 (Ware Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Similar to 1.a3, this advances a flank pawn without contesting the center. Black seizes central space, preparing quick development (e.g., Nf6, Bc5), punishing White's inefficiency in top theory.
  • 1. b3 (Nimzowitsch-Larsen Attack): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: White prepares Bb2 but concedes central initiative. Black grabs the center, often leading to transpositions into favorable lines where Black's space advantage is key in GM play.
  • 1. b4 (Polish/Sokolsky Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: This grabs space on the queenside but weakens b4. Black counters in the center, attacking the b4 pawn indirectly and achieving comfortable development, as seen in rare high-level encounters.
  • 1. c3 (Saragossa Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: A slow move supporting d4 later, but Black occupies the center first, preventing easy expansion and leading to solid positions like a reversed Caro-Kann, equalizing effortlessly.
  • 1. c4 (English Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Mirrors White's flank approach but gains a tempo in reversed Sicilian structures. Popular at super-GM levels for dynamic counterplay and avoiding symmetrical boredom.
  • 1. d3 (Mieses Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Modest central advance; Black mirrors and controls more space, preparing Nf6 and e5, achieving equality or better due to White's delayed development.
  • 1. d4 (Queen's Pawn Opening): Black's best: 1...Nf6 (Indian Defense). Why: Flexible and hypermodern, prevents e4 while preparing ...d5 or ...g6. Dominant in top tournaments for its rich strategic depth and counterattacking potential.
  • 1. e3 (Van't Kruijs Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Slow and non-committal; Black takes the center, often transposing to Queen's Pawn games where Black equalizes easily, as White hasn't claimed space.
  • 1. e4 (King's Pawn Opening): Black's best: 1...c5 (Sicilian Defense). Why: Aggressive asymmetrical response challenging the center indirectly. Favored in world championships for queenside counterplay and complexity, though engines like Stockfish also approve 1...e6.
  • 1. f3 (Barnes Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Weakens kingside severely; Black occupies the center, preparing attacks (e.g., Nf6, Bc5+). Theory deems this punishable, leading to Black advantage in correct play.
  • 1. f4 (Bird's Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Controls e4 and supports ...Nf6. Solid at high levels, often transposing to Dutch Defense reversed, where Black's central presence neutralizes White's fianchetto ideas.
  • 1. g3 (Benko Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Prepares fianchetto but ignores center; Black occupies it, leading to comfortable middlegames. Common in theory for its simplicity and equality.
  • 1. g4 (Grob's Attack): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Aggressively weakens kingside; Black attacks the g4 pawn (Bg2 hangs after ...Bxg4 later). Engines rate this as bad for White, and correct play gives Black a clear edge.
  • 1. h3 (Clemenz Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Pointless flank move; Black seizes center, developing quickly. High-level theory treats this as inferior, allowing Black easy play.
  • 1. h4 (Desprez Opening): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Similar to 1.h3 but advances further, weakening kingside. Black controls center, preparing castling and potential attacks.
  • 1. Na3 (Sodium Attack): Black's best: 1...e5. Why: Develops knight to edge, wasting tempo. Black takes center, ignoring the misplaced knight, leading to superior development in theory.
  • 1. Nc3 (Van Geet Opening): Black's best: 1...Nf6. Why: Flexible knight development; can transpose to Vienna or other lines. Solid at elite levels for controlling key squares.
  • 1. Nf3 (Réti Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Hypermodern but allows transpositions. Black occupies center directly, often leading to Queen's Gambit or Slav setups, equalizing well in GM practice.
  • 1. Nh3 (Amar Opening): Black's best: 1...d5. Why: Poor knight placement; Black controls center, preparing ...e5 or Nf6. Theory considers this weak, giving Black an early initiative.
